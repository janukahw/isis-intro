Scene 02 — the machine

First, make protons. Fast ones.

It starts as hydrogen gas. Ions are accelerated down a linac (a straight-line accelerator), then around a synchrotron ring 163 m in circumference — at injection the spare electrons are stripped off, leaving bare protons that gain energy each lap until they’re kicked out toward the metal targets where the neutrons and muons are made (next scene).

800 MeVmega-electronvolts of proton energy — 84% of the speed of light, one lap every 0.65 µs

Scene 03 — two probes for the price of one

The beam makes muons, then neutrons.

On the way to Target Station 1 the protons first pass through a 1 cm slice of graphite, where collisions make short-lived particles called pions, which fall apart (‘decay’) within metres into muons — heavy, unstable cousins of the electron. Graphite is thin and light, so most protons punch straight through; the surviving beam then slams into a thick tungsten target, chipping neutrons off its heavy, neutron-packed nuclei. That process is called spallation.

~10–15neutrons knocked out per incoming proton

Scene 04 — slow down

Fresh neutrons are uselessly fast.

Straight off the target they move at tens of thousands of km/s — far too fast to probe atoms. So they pass through a moderator: a small tank of water, liquid methane or liquid hydrogen. Quantum mechanics’ strange gift: anything that moves also behaves as a wave — the slower it moves, the longer its wavelength. A few dozen bounces later they emerge thousands of times slower, with wavelengths that match the spacing between atoms.

≈ 1/10,000thof their speed left after moderation — and that’s the point

Scene 05 — the race that measures itself

Every pulse is a race down the beamline.

All the neutrons in a pulse start together. Remember: slower = longer wave. So the short-wavelength ones are fast; the long-wavelength ones lag. By the time they reach the sample (the sliver of material a researcher has parked in the beam to study), arrival time tells you the wavelength — the facility’s clock is its most important instrument.

Scene 06 — the data is born

Detectors don’t see pictures. They see events.

Neutrons scatter off the sample and hit banks of detectors. Each hit is recorded as a tiny log line: (detector ID, time of flight). That’s the raw data — no images, no spectra, just an enormous, append-only event log.

10⁶–10⁹events in the file from one ‘run’ (a single measurement, minutes to hours)

Interlude — scenes 02–06 in one picture

One pulse, end to end.

Fifty times a second: protons accelerate, shed muons at the graphite, make neutrons in the tungsten, slow down in the moderator, then race down the beamline — and every detector hit becomes one logged event.

Scene 07 — from log file to physics

Reduction turns events into answers.

Software — at ISIS, mainly Mantid — aggregates the events, converts clock-time into physical units, divides out the machine’s fingerprint (the source delivers more neutrons at some wavelengths than others — dividing by a reference run removes that bias) and fits a model. Out the other end: numbers with error bars that go straight into a paper.
